## Wed 2026-06-17 - The quiet shift of 2026: agent memory hit production

- **X thread (3) 18:41 IST** · **LinkedIn 09:30 IST** · no hashtags

**X**

1. Something shifted quietly in AI this year and I don't think enough people clocked it. Agent memory moved from research demos into production. The tooling for it all matured at once, and persistent memory started landing inside the big assistants.
2. Working with it closely, the pattern that keeps winning is layered: a small working memory in the moment, a searchable recall layer for what matters, cold storage for the rest. The difference between a tool that resets every morning and one that compounds.
3. The gap I keep observing: almost none of it carries governance. No lineage for where a fact came from, no clean way to forget it, no resolution when two memories disagree. Remembering is getting solved. Trusting the memory isn't. That's the real prize.

**LinkedIn**

Something shifted quietly in AI this year, and I do not think enough people clocked it. Agent memory moved from research demos into production. The tooling for it matured almost at once, and persistent memory started landing inside the big assistant platforms.

I've been working with this closely, and the pattern that keeps winning is layered. A small working memory in the moment, a searchable recall layer for what actually matters, and cold storage for the rest. It is the difference between a tool that resets every morning and one that compounds the longer you use it.

But here is the gap I keep observing. Almost none of these memory layers come with governance. No lineage for where a fact came from, no clean way to forget it, no resolution when two stored memories disagree. Remembering is getting solved fast. Trusting that memory is not. The teams that crack the second part, not just the first, are the ones who will build something people do not want to switch away from.

## Fri 2026-06-19 - I stopped writing prompts and started handing over problems

- **X thread (5) 17:44 IST** · **LinkedIn 09:21 IST** · no hashtags

**X**

1. I changed how I work with AI over the last few months and only really clocked it this week: I stopped writing prompts. I started handing over problems.
2. The old way was coaxing. Clever instructions, careful examples, the right phrasing to nudge a model toward what I wanted.
3. The new way, with models that actually reason, is closer to briefing a sharp new hire. I give it the real problem, the constraints I care about, the reference material, and the edges where I already hold an opinion.
4. Then I let it work, and I push back on what comes out, the same way I would with a person. The back-and-forth is where the quality shows up, not the opening prompt.
5. What I observed across real tasks: quality now tracks the quality of my brief, not the cleverness of my prompt. The skill isn't prompt-whispering anymore. It's knowing the problem cold and telling good from almost-good. Still entirely human, and worth more now.

## Sun 2026-06-21 - Substack: the year the bottleneck moved (published manually)

- **Publish about 18:24 IST.** Teasers Mon 2026-06-22 (X 09:34 IST, LinkedIn 19:02 IST) linking the piece. No hashtags.

**Title:** The year the bottleneck moved

**Body**

I spent this week paying attention to one question: where the hard part of AI actually lives now. A few moments made it concrete.

I had two and a half days with Fable before it was pulled, and I gave it a real design problem instead of a benchmark. What stood out was not the output, it was that it reasoned from principles. Capability is no longer the question.

Then the numbers landed. Close to 9 in 10 enterprise AI agent pilots never reach production, and the reasons are almost never the model: unclear goals, agents that cannot reach their tools and data, evaluations that drift under real inputs. The bottleneck moved from the model to everything around it.

Meanwhile, the quietest big shift of the year: agent memory went from research to production. The tooling matured, the layered pattern settled. But almost none of it carries governance, where a fact came from, how to forget it, what to do when two memories disagree.

Put those together and the picture is clear to me. The model stopped being the moat. What is left is the unglamorous part: trust, a memory you can stand behind, and the judgment to know when the confident answer is wrong. That is far harder than a clever demo, and it is the only part I find genuinely worth building right now.
