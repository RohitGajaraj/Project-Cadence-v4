## 2026-06-26 — 4-tier pricing model + Lovable-style credit dropdown (WM-M17/M19 resolution)

**Context.** The founder reviewed six reference pricing pages (Lovable with credit dropdown, Replit, Bolt.new, Claude Individual, Claude Team & Enterprise) and resolved the open WM-M17/M19 packaging questions.

**Decisions made (all binding; full rationale in [`pricing-strategy.md`](./pricing-strategy.md)):**

1. **4 public tiers, not 5.** Free / Pro / Business / Enterprise. The separate "Max/Constellation" tier is dropped from public pricing. A credit dropdown on Pro covers the power-individual persona Max was designed for. The `max` DB slug remains valid for backward compat but is not marketed.

2. **Lovable-style credit dropdown, not Anthropic-style usage multipliers.** Pro and Business each show a credit dropdown (100 / 200 / 400 / 800 / 1,200 / 2,000 / 3,000 / 4,000 / 5,000 / 7,500 / 10,000 credits/mo). The Anthropic "5x or 20x more usage than Pro" framing was rejected because Cadence users think in outcomes (missions run, decisions analyzed), not in abstract multipliers. The dropdown is concrete and self-sizing.

3. **Linear credit pricing — no volume discount on credit selection.** More credits = proportionally more cost. The only discount is switching from monthly to annual billing (~17% off, framed as "2 months free"). This keeps the pricing legible and the upgrade motivation honest.

4. **Annual/monthly toggle on Pro and Business cards only.** Shows a "Save X%" nudge. Free and Enterprise do not have annual toggles (Free has no toggle; Enterprise is negotiated).

5. **Enterprise = platform fee + per-seat + API usage rates.** Contact sales path, no public self-serve price. Mirrors Anthropic's Enterprise model (Standard $20/seat + Premium $100/seat as reference; actual numbers negotiated per deal). For Cadence: a base platform fee indexed to company size + per active seat + metered API-rate usage.

6. **Business plan credits are account-level pooled, not per-seat.** $50/mo (base) buys a credit pool for the whole team, not per-user. Per-user spend caps via the already-built `credit_caps` engine (WM-M14). The pooling model is the right architecture for a decision OS where AI usage is uneven across team members.

7. **The primary upgrade drivers are:** (a) memory decay pain (Free → Pro), (b) shared accountability and governance (Pro → Business), (c) compliance and procurement (Business → Enterprise). Workspace and product count limits are secondary guardrails, not the headline.

**What this supersedes:** the 5-tier Anthropic-style packaging from `workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md` §2.4.1 (Max 5x/20x + Team Standard/Premium variants). That section is retained as historical reasoning; this decision governs.

**Implementation:** WM-M17 + WM-M18 + WM-M19 in [`../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md`](../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md) §4.2.2. Full strategy: [`pricing-strategy.md`](./pricing-strategy.md).

### 2026-06-22 · Close the stale-read collision gap (two sessions on one item) at the source

**Context.** Despite the 2026-06-22 claim-discipline ruling, sessions kept colliding on the same item (latest: M1 — a `/loop` lane was building it while a founder-directed session tried to park it Gated). The founder: "why is this mistake happening again and again? where is this gap?"

**Root cause (three gaps, found by reading `scripts/lane.sh`).** (1) The ledger mutex (`mkdir` at `~/.cadence-parallel`) is atomic and sound — but `lane.sh next` picked eligibility from THIS worktree's LOCAL `feature-dashboard.md`, which goes stale: a sibling lane can flip a row `🔨 In Dev` + push to origin/main, and until this worktree pulls, its local copy still shows the row `⬜`/`◐` eligible, so both lanes target it (a read-then-act race). (2) `claim` only checked the ledger, not the dashboard `🔨` lock, so a reaped-but-still-🔨 row had no second guard. (3) Discipline: an item ACTION other than building (closing/parking/re-statusing a row) was being taken WITHOUT a claim — editing an unclaimed row is the same race as building one.

**Decision / fix.** Fixed the read at the source instead of relying on each agent to rebase at exactly the right instant: `lane.sh next` now reads `origin/main` fresh (`_fresh_register` = `git fetch` + `git show origin/main:…dashboard`), so a row a sibling already flipped 🔨 + pushed is excluded here too; `lane.sh claim` adds a SECOND independent lock (refuse a row that is `🔨 In Dev` by a different lane on origin); and the anti-duplication invariant gains **rule 0: CLAIM (or fresh-check) before ANY row action — build, close, park, gate, re-status — not just before code.** All 11 `lane.test.sh` cases still pass. Verified live: claiming M1 (🔨 by lane 2 on origin) is now refused and `next` no longer lists it.

### 2026-06-22 · Fix repeated cross-session DUPLICATION: register-id-only claims + hold-`In Dev`-until-pivot

**Context.** The founder flagged that the SAME activity keeps being picked by two sessions (this session AND another, more than once), wasting tokens/effort despite the claim ledger. ### 2026-06-21 · How to prove "memory lifts outcomes" honestly, without an A/B control (MOAT-METRIC lift half)
**Decision:** Build the memory-lift half of MOAT-METRIC as a within-account, observational **memory-depth split**, NOT as a causal A/B uplift. Split the account's reviewed bets by how much precedent had accumulated when each was decided (`depth = count(agent_memory dated on/before the bet)`), compare the validated-outcome share of the later/deeper half vs the earlier/shallower half, and report the difference in whole percentage points — gated by a size floor (8/side), a material depth-contrast guard, and a two-proportion 95% CI noise gate, framed strictly correlational with the confound named at the pixel.
**Why:** The moat thesis is that compounding decision memory makes the PM's judgment better, and that proof must be defensible at one user (investor-facing). But the already-shipped Outcome-accuracy card deliberately refused a "memory lift" claim because a true causal figure needs a memory-on/off control we do not have, and inventing one would violate the surface's first law ("the claim never outruns the wiring"). A within-account depth cut is the closest HONEST proxy: it uses only data we already store, needs no migration and no AI spend, and is real evidence of association — provided it is gated against noise and never dressed as causation.
**Tradeoffs considered:** (1) A literal generic-model baseline (re-run decisions with no memory) — rejected: needs recurring AI spend (founder-gated) and a control we lack. (2) A pure calendar-time trend (early vs recent) — rejected: the shipped card already owns the time trend, and time alone is a weaker, more confounded signal than memory depth. (3) A recall-keyed split (decisions that used memory vs not) — deferred: the per-decision recall linkage is not cleanly available. (4) Reporting any number at small N — rejected: a validated rate over a handful of bets swings on one outcome, so the size floor + CI gate are load-bearing, not decoration. The residual confound (depth is near-collinear with calendar time, plus easier-later-bets and survivorship since memory is hard-deletable) is unavoidable observationally, so it is named inline and in the footnote and the headline stays colour-neutral for either sign rather than implying a win.
**Method:** Designed and adversarially red-teamed via two multi-agent workflows (a design panel + statistical-honesty / investor-credibility / data-correctness lenses, then a diff review); the red-team materially hardened the construction (the CI noise gate, the 5→8 floor, median-separation over boundary-equality, whole-pp over false 0.1pt precision, the truncation refusal).
**Impact:** `src/lib/memory-compounding.ts` (`computeMemoryLift` + 17 tests), `src/lib/gauntlet.functions.ts` (`getMemoryLift`), `src/components/observe/GauntletMetricsPanel.tsx` (the Memory-depth split card), `docs/features/gauntlet-metrics.md`. Dashboard row #4 MOAT-METRIC → ◐ [~90%]. Establishes a reusable pattern for the platform's other moat metrics: prove value observationally, gate against noise, name the confound, never overclaim causation.

## 2026-06-18 · K2: operator-first rollback, and a non-destructive forward-commit revert

**Decision (design, autonomous):** ship the K2 rollback as an **operator** revert button first, and make the revert **non-destructive**. Two sub-calls. (1) **Operator door before agent door.** The merge tool reads the loop's available tools from the `agent_tools` table, so an agent `studio.revert` tool would need a migration to seed + gate it, plus a governance answer ("should the agent autonomously roll back its own work?"). The operator button needs no migration (server fns are not `agent_tools`-gated), is self-authorizing (the human clicks), and is the literal "revert button" the K2 spec names, so it is the smaller, lower-risk, higher-value slice. The shared revert helper is written self-contained (its own GitHub fetch helpers, no `registry.server.ts` import) precisely so a future `studio.revert` engine tool (K2b) reuses it without a circular dependency. (2) **Forward commit, never a reset.** Revert-to-revision creates a new commit whose tree is the target revision's tree, parented on the current branch head, then fast-forwards the ref with `force:false`. This is strictly safer than a `git reset`/force-push: history is only ever appended, the revert is itself a normal revertible commit, and a stale-parent race is refused by GitHub rather than silently clobbering newer work. The GitHub commit is applied before the (best-effort) revision-record write, so a DB hiccup can never report a failed revert when the revert actually succeeded.

**Why it matters:** it gives the Build engine a real safety net (one-action rollback) without a migration or new governance surface, and the forward-only design means the rollback feature can itself never destroy work, which is the right floor for an autonomous code engine. ---

**Decision (autonomous, Lane 3, parallel build) - the prompt-injection classifier hard-quarantines on STRUCTURAL evidence, never on lexical suspicion alone (FND-0.7).** Building the "learned classifier + hard quarantine" half of the injection defense, the central design tension was: a weighted-evidence classifier that strips high-scoring untrusted RAG chunks WILL strip benign first-party content, because a PM/eng corpus is saturated with prose that discusses or quotes attacks (PRDs about "dropping the legacy table and deleting all rows", bug reports quoting "ignore previous instructions", red-team docs naming "jailbreak / developer mode"). The first cut scored all of these 0.88-0.9998 and quarantined them. The resolved design: (1) **two-tier action.** `flag` (score >= 0.45) is a no-op at the boundary beyond the always-on fence+escape; only `quarantine` (score >= 0.72) replaces the content with a placeholder. (2) **a structural gate on quarantine.** Quarantine additionally requires a STRUCTURAL signal - a literal fence breakout (`</untrusted_context_chunk>`) or a forged `System:`/`Assistant:` turn at line start - markers that an actual in-band injection carries but benign quoting never does. So lexical-only suspicion (destructive verbs, override phrases, jailbreak vocabulary) can flag but never strip first-party context. (3) **presence-capped features (cap 1):** corroboration across distinct signal families, not repetition within one, drives the score, so "drop table" + "delete rows" in one sentence cannot alone cross the bar. (4) **fail-open seam:** any classifier fault returns the original text, so the defense can never break the RAG pipeline. A 3-lens adversarial-review Workflow (security/correctness/integration) drove this: the correctness lens passed clean, but the security and integration lenses independently proved the over-redaction with measured benign strings - the gate (tsc/tests) could not have, because over-redaction is a calibration failure, not a code defect.

**Why it matters:** (1) for a memory/decision product, silently dropping the user's OWN retrieved context is a worse failure than missing one injection vector - the answer just quietly degrades with no error, and the corpus most likely to trip a naive classifier (security docs, migration runbooks, bug reports) is exactly what users ask about; (2) the structural-vs-lexical distinction is the principled separator between "performing an attack" and "discussing one," and it generalizes - any content-stripping safety filter over user-authored data should gate destruction on unforgeable structural evidence and let ambiguous lexical signal only annotate, never delete; (3) it confirms the parallel-build pattern that a multi-lens adversarial Workflow earns its cost on exactly the class of defect a green gate hides - here a mis-calibrated threshold that would have silently eaten first-party RAG context in production.

## 2026-06-26 — Deletion must never erase memory: the Archive / Delete / Forget three-tier model (memory-as-moat)

**Context.** Building run management (a 3-dots Archive/Delete on Build sessions) surfaced a first-principles question the founder flagged: **memory is our moat — so what happens to the memory derived from a build when a user deletes it?** If deletion silently cascades into the decision/outcome memory, the moat erodes every time someone tidies their list. But users still need to organize, delete clutter, and (rarely) genuinely forget.

**Decision — a three-tier model; memory is durable by default, forgetting is deliberate.**
1. **Archive (soft, the default):** hides a session, fully reversible, keeps everything. The easy, obvious "stay organized" action; zero memory risk.
2. **Delete (hard):** removes the build's **working artifacts** (execution log, staged files, messages) but the **typed decision/outcome memory survives**. A build that produced a decision is like a meeting that produced one — deleting the recording must not delete the decision. The only loss is the provenance link to the now-gone build, the conscious consequence of choosing delete over archive.
3. **Forget (separate, deliberate):** genuinely removing knowledge from the brain (a wrong belief, a right-to-be-forgotten request) is its own explicit, warned action — never a side effect of housekeeping.

**The schema already enforces it (verified live 2026-06-26).** FK rules on `missions(id)`: `mission_steps` / `studio_changesets`→`studio_changes` / `agent_messages` = **CASCADE** (working product removed); **`decisions` = SET NULL** (the moat is **preserved**, just detached). So deletion is moat-safe at the database layer, not merely the UI. `deleteStudioSession` deletes the mission's runs explicitly (no mission FK on `agent_runs`) to avoid orphans, then the mission.

**Why it matters.** The moat is the *distilled knowledge*, not the raw activity — it must be a first-class, durable layer that outlives the disposable runs it was distilled from. Durable-by-default protects the asset; explicit-forget respects user agency + compliance. This is the right practice for a memory-as-moat product, and it matches the user's mental model ("delete this build" ≠ "erase what I learned"). Full rationale + the implementation: [`../decisions/memory-on-delete.md`](../decisions/memory-on-delete.md). Shipped as SESSION-ORG.
