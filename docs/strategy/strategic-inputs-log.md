## 2026-06-19 - Credit engine + the market evidence for account-level pooling (companion to the tenancy/monetization entry below)

**Trigger.** A parallel founder brainstorm, run alongside the workspace/tenancy session below, on the monetization model from a pure user + business lens: a user does not want to bring keys from Claude / Antigravity / others, so the platform should consolidate all the frontier models behind a single **credits** abstraction; the front end shows credits, the back end clubs every managed cost (inference, DB, auth, tool calls) into that one unit. The founder wanted the credit model designed end-to-end, an implicit-not-coercive lock-in, a low-friction free-to-paid funnel, and an explicit call on whether billing/credits sit at the workspace level (Lovable-style) or pool higher. The two sessions were then reconciled into one source of truth.

**The resolution (distilled into [`session-decisions.md`](./session-decisions.md) and the credit engine §4.2.1 of [`../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md`](../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md)).** Four product calls, then the engine: (1) **credits = plan + a generous included monthly allowance + cheap capped top-ups**, not a pure pay-as-you-go wallet, so the meter stays calm and abundant; (2) **the free tier pulls on two hooks at once**, a starter credit grant (enough to feel the loop work) plus memory that decays on a rolling 30 days, because losing the accrued second brain is the deeper pull; (3) **billing pools at the account**, confirmed against the tenancy session (the founder initially described account-level pooling himself, briefly detoured to a two-tier workspace-as-biller framing on my faulty recon, then we aligned back, see the reconciliation note); (4) **no self-serve BYOK**, managed credits are the only self-serve path, aligning with the same-day ruling below. The **engine** that powers this: a credit is a stable unit abstracting blended COGS, `creditsForCost` converts the already-captured `ai_events.est_cost_usd` to credits with margin living in grant-sizing (not the per-credit price); the debit draws down included-then-top-up and halts cleanly when empty; the pool is the account with per-product / per-member attribution and optional caps; margin is held by cost-aware model routing + caching. A deliberate UX move unique to this session: a **legibility layer** (approximate per-action credit ranges, "a PRD draft is about 40 to 120 credits") so the user can read what things cost without a raw per-call meter, which is the calm-front doctrine applied to billing.

**Competitive grounding (the market benchmark that confirms the account-level call).** This was the evidence that settled the workspace-vs-account billing question. Lovable's per-project billing is the **outlier**, and the wrong comp, because it is a build tool, not a memory-compounding decision OS, so its per-project meter taxes the exact behavior we want to encourage. Every platform whose value compounds with usage pools at the org/account level and treats the sub-container as **cost attribution, not a separate bill**: Anthropic (Organization to Workspaces), OpenAI (Organization to Projects), Vercel/v0, Bolt (account token pool), Replit Pro (pooled). The per-seat-per-workspace billers, Linear and Notion, deliberately do the opposite, but they have no per-workspace moat (a workspace there is an admin silo, the inverse of our case). The decisive framing: **per-workspace billing creates a perverse incentive**, it charges users to weaken our own moat (more workspaces and products are more compounding memory), so they consolidate and the silos thin. Account-level pooling makes "more usage = deeper moat" a literal property of the schema.

**The reconciliation (two threads into one source of truth), and the vocabulary trap that caused the detour.** This session ran in parallel to the tenancy session and initially proposed a competing structure (a two-tier "workspace is the billing entity" model) and keeping BYOK, both because the first recon missed the already-written build bible. The founder caught it. Root cause was a **vocabulary trap**: the word "workspace" means the billing entity in Cadence's schema but means "the thing you have many of" in Lovable, so "credits across workspaces" read two different ways. Once the built plan surfaced, we aligned to its three-tier account-level model and its BYOK-removal ruling, and **merged the two threads into the one build bible** (the credit engine now lives in §4.2.1, no longer a separate doc). The operating lesson, logged for future sessions: before proposing structure, find the parallel session's artifact; the recon must not conclude "no such doc" from a narrow search.

**Why this is investor-grade.** Credits-not-seats is the monetization that grows as decisioning automates (you charge for the decision work, which expands, not for headcount, which shrinks); account-level pooling is the market-proven pattern for value-compounds-with-usage products and is the schema-level expression of the moat; and the legibility layer shows monetization can be calm and honest rather than anxiety-inducing, which is itself a trust and retention lever. The full credit-engine build spec (cross-tool portable, any tool can pick up a `WM-M10` to `WM-M16` ID and build it cold) is §4.2.1 of the build bible.

## 2026-06-19 - Workspace, accounts, tenancy + monetization (and how lock-in should actually work)

**Trigger.** A founder brainstorm: the workspace layer was vaguely designed, with no thought given to switching, cross-impact, settings, ownership transfer, or what a new user first sees. The founder wanted a tighter model (how Lovable lets users manage projects, but better), the moat made explicit, an implicit (never coercive) lock-in, a seeded showcase for new users and investors/YC, and a cross-tool implementation plan so work can continue on Antigravity/Lovable when Claude Code limits hit.

**The resolution (distilled into [`session-decisions.md`](./session-decisions.md) and [`../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md`](../planning/workspace-tenancy-and-monetization-plan.md)).** Three levels: Account > Workspace > Product. Billing and the credit pool sit at the **account**, not the workspace, because per-workspace billing taxes the one thing that makes the product un-leaveable. The deepest argument, reconciled with the parallel monetization session: **the strongest lock-in is gravity, not a wall.** A team that has run the product for months has a workspace that knows its product like a senior teammate; a full data export does not export the tuned judgment + accrued decision memory, so you can keep exit easy (which raises trust and word of mouth) and still be effectively un-leaveable. That flips a design rule: the more workspaces a user spins up under one account, the more memory compounds, the deeper the moat, so account-level pooling beats Lovable-style per-project billing friction. Memory persistence is the primary charge lever (free decays on a rolling 30 days, paid persists); usage credits ride on top and protect margin via generous allowance + cheap fair-use top-ups, because the all-in-one one-subscription model now eats both LLM and hosting COGS. **Managed AI credits become the only self-serve path; user-supplied BYOK is removed from self-serve and kept enterprise-only.** Model-agnostic routing across providers (our keys) is preserved as the principle; only USER keys are retired. This is a deliberate pivot from "BYOK primary" (a same-day refinement of an interim "BYOK optional" stance), cross-corrected across the README, the constitution, and the architecture docs. The deeper reposition this session: **the moat is the decision layer; memory is one layer of it** (vibe-coding owns "how to build", which commoditizes; we own "what to build and was it right", which does not). Full articulation, competition map, and the YC objection Q&A: [`moat.md`](./moat.md).

**Naming + identity grounding.** Tier names are a presentation skin over stable slugs (`free|pro|max|team|enterprise`), so they can be renamed anytime with no migration, which matters while the product name itself ("Cadence") is a placeholder. The musical-notes direction was rejected (Kimi Code already owns it). The chosen theme, **Constellation** (Star/Cluster/Constellation/Galaxy/Cosmos), names tiers after what the product does to your knowledge: it connects scattered points into a navigable map that gets richer with use, so the name is the value and the depth is something you can picture. The motif is an ownable animated starfield glyph that gains stars/links/glow per tier (distinct from Kimi's notes and Claude's tree), respecting reduced-motion.

**Why this is investor-grade.** The argued case: workspace structure is where the moat and the lock-in physically live. Scoping decision memory to the workspace/account turns the abstract "memory moat" into an enforced, per-tenant, compounding data fact, and the account-level pooling makes "more usage = deeper moat" a literal property of the schema. Lock-in by value (not hostage) is simultaneously the retention engine and the evangelization engine, because portability-by-trust lowers churn while the brain that does not travel keeps people in.

## 2026-06-18 - Agent experience brainstorm (resolving "19 vs 6", and how a multi-agent system should look)

**Trigger.** A founder brainstorm: our strategy says "19+ agents that drill down to 6, with the 19 supporting in the backend," but it was never clear how that resolves in the product, and the platform shows leftover/inconsistent agents. The founder's questions: should we expose all the agents to the end user, or wire them in the backend and show "the right agent is being called, then handed off to the next" as it happens? How do we clean this up, keep it un-overwhelming yet show the full engine, and seed agents at every phase (not just Build)? Plus: refine the roster (club/add), tighten prompts to deliver real value, and get naming + visual identity modern and relatable (not technical: "people don't relate to 'Discovery'").

**The resolution (distilled into [`session-decisions.md`](./session-decisions.md) and [`../features/agent-experience.md`](../features/agent-experience.md)).** The number you expose is zero. 19+ = the engine's growable specialist catalog (moat capacity, never a menu). 6 stations = the map the user navigates. 5 faces = the cast shown only in motion. Expose the relay, not the roster. Cast (visible in motion) vs Crew (engine-only). This is the Engine-Room Doctrine applied to agents.

**Competitive grounding (how leaders surface agents, late 2025-2026).** Three UI philosophies, mapped to the user: (A) explicit DAG / node-canvas = the engineer's control room (LangGraph Studio's auto-edge "hairball"; LangChain had to add a separate "Chat mode" for non-engineers); (B) hidden graph behind one calm narrative pane (Manus's "Computer" runs hundreds of parallel agents and draws zero of them; ChatGPT agent's "virtual computer"); (C) calm gallery of configured agents with the machinery one click down (Google Gemini Enterprise Agent Gallery, Notion Custom Agents, Linear). No category leader ships a live roster of running bots as the primary surface. The dev-agent world converged on a status-grouped Kanban "command center" (Devin's Agent Command Center, GitHub mission control, Cursor cloud), but each agent collapses to one card + one status pill, with the control room one click down. The strongest reusable pattern for "N agents in one space without noise" is Linear's typed, ephemeral activity stream (each agent shows only its latest line, self-replacing) plus LukeW's collapse-to-summary (when results land, the process column collapses to a one-line summary + link and focus shifts to the artifact). Anti-patterns: the live DAG as the front door; the data-dump of every log line ("notification blindness"); a roster of running bots with internals exposed + per-step approval modals (Cursor 2.0's chrome was called "absurd" by its own users). The show-vs-hide tension is genuinely unresolved in the field (LukeW), so the safe stance is calm-by-default, depth one click away, user-controllable.

**Naming + identity grounding.** Modern serious B2B is moving away from human first-names (they over-promise human competence and now read as "AI slop"; "Elara" was named an AI-favorite name of 2025). Raw role/mechanism names ("Discovery", "Analyst") are cold and violate the outcome-naming law. The live winner is the plain-spoken archetype (Microsoft shipped an always-on agent called "Scout" in mid-2026). Iconography: give each agent a visual identity, but an abstract monogram/glyph, not a face (faces over-anthropomorphize; the Linear/Claude/Copilot pattern). Per-agent color must not collide with the reserved status semantics (ember/green/blue/red). The called-out treatment is mark + name + present-tense verb + outcome; identity always disclosed; the human stays accountable; reasoning one click away (Linear's Agent Interaction Guidelines).

**Why this is investor-grade.** The argued case: memory + a legible, governed multi-agent loop (not raw autonomy and not a bot roster) is the defensible product. Code has a fast oracle and "what to build" does not, so the moat is the remembered outcome of decisions, and the UX that makes a swarm legible-yet-calm is the felt differentiator. Full competitive + naming research (~40 cited URLs: LukeW lukew.com/ff 2106 + 2142, Smashing Magazine's 2026 agentic-UX + transparency pieces, NN/g 2026, Google PAIR, Linear's Agent Interaction Guidelines, and the Devin/Cursor/Manus/Notion/Gemini-Enterprise changelogs) is preserved in this session's research deliverables.

>

It's directionally right that "what to build" becomes the bottleneck once "how to build" is cheap, that's just conservation of bottleneck. ### Part B - the build-workflow, audit, and build-next synthesis

Distilled into [`v9`](./v9-decision-wedge-and-build-next.md) sections 4, 5, 6. Key inputs preserved here:

- **The end-to-end loop is largely built and seamless.** Signal (webhook KI-10) to Decide (Scout cluster, ICE re-score, Critic red-team, decision card F-DEC-CARD) to Define (cited PRD H1, Critic-on-spec DEF-03) to Build (the tiered engine: branch isolation I3, per-hunk curation I1, live cockpit I2, tests J1, CI gate J2, release notes K1) to Learn (outcome to memory W1). The user touches it at exactly **two governance gates** (the decision call, the merge approval), not a dozen juggling points. The PRD-to-coding-mission seam is already one approval (`dispatchStudioSession`, with a `prd -> mission` lineage edge).
- **The two real gaps are capability gaps, not friction gaps:** (A) sandbox + live preview so self-correction is a real loop action, not CI polling (v8 Phase 3); (B) real predicted-vs-actual outcome reviews (LRN-02) + analytics (F-ANALYTICS), which is what actually closes the loop and compounds the moat. Gap B is the #1 highest-leverage unbuilt work.
- **Honest audit verdict:** the engine (G0, 11/11) and the Build spine (G3, complete) are real and strong; the *ends* of the loop (real ingestion, real outcome learning) and the *packaging* (the wedge, onboarding, interop) are the unfinished, highest-leverage work. The last mile is the moat; closing it is the work, not catch-up.
- **Build-next, tiered by leverage:** T1 close the loop on real data (SEN-01 second ingest, LRN-02 outcome reviews); T2 package the Critic-teardown wedge (W6 onboarding, PLG funnel, F-SHARE as the shareable teardown); T3 a thin read-only MCP surface (slice of Q1, pulled forward from M-D); T4 sandbox/preview + the BYO-key delegate-out (v8 Phase 3, BLD-04).

### Part C - the autonomous-engine + market-evidence synthesis (after a founder challenge)

The founder challenged the Part A "refuse to build the coding engine" call and reframed the 80/20 as *agent-vs-human*. A market check followed. The result is the current canon ([`v9`](./v9-decision-wedge-and-build-next.md) sections 1 and 4).

- **Own the autonomous engine - yes - with one precision.** For PM-shaped work (a new field, a screen, a workflow, a CRUD feature, copy, an integration), autonomous coding is viable today and the engine is already built. Do **not** aspire to be "Cursor": the hardest 20% (deep refactors, ambiguous evolving work, IDE ergonomics for senior engineers) is where even Devin still fails most and where a believable 2-week build becomes 12 months. Own the autonomous *delivery* engine; make delegate-out a **BYO-key escape hatch** (customer's own Cursor/Devin/Claude key via the model-agnostic chokepoint, so no revenue split and no margin leak; the default path stays 100% in-platform and 100% revenue).
- **The autonomous loop IS the product, and the design rule that resolves "full autonomy" vs "ambient + governed":** push autonomy hardest where there is an oracle (code has tests/CI, so BUILD to QA to SHIP can run fully autonomous, self-correcting against ground truth) and keep it governed where there is none (product judgment, so DECIDE stays human-gated). That is the precise form of "agents execute, humans govern."
- **The decisive moat argument:** task-spec quality is the #1 determinant of autonomous-coding success (Devin ~67% PR-merge on well-scoped work vs ~15-30% on vague/evolving work). A standalone Cursor/Devin gets a thin ticket; Cadence feeds *its own* engine a signal-grounded, Critic-hardened, cited PRD. Owning both ends means the build engine runs on better input than anyone else's can, a moat a bolt-on can never have because it requires owning the upstream.
- **Market grounding (sources):** developers run *multiple* coding tools and re-evaluate every 2 to 4 months; the stickiness is to the engineer's IDE, not the PM's surface, so the PM buyer is not locked to Cursor and value wins ([multi-tool developer behavior](https://www.sitepoint.com/claude-code-vs-cursor-vs-copilot-the-2026-developer-comparison/)). Autonomous coding of well-scoped work is viable now but reliability lags on ambiguous work ([Devin reliability 2026](https://www.idlen.io/blog/devin-ai-engineer-review-limits-2026/); [Cognition agent-first bet](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317354/20260529/ai-coding-agents-cognitions-26b-raise-bets-agent-first-architecture-beats-ide-tools.htm)). SaaS consolidation is a 2026 tailwind: ~93 apps per company, a McKinsey "consolidation wave" cutting SaaS 20 to 35% by 2027, ~70% of firms consolidating, CFOs demanding ROI on renewals ([SaaS consolidation 2026](https://vantagepoint.io/blog/sf/insights/platform-consolidation-2026-saas-stack-reduction-ai)).

## 2026-06-17 - "2026 is the year of the agent manager" (operating-model frame)

**Trigger.** A founder brainstorm prompt: 2026 is the year of "the agent manager." Work shifts from creating everything yourself to directing, reviewing, and orchestrating multiple agents; the operating model changes before the job titles do. The founder's instinct: make the agent-manager role explicit in the product (and on the landing page), with a place that shows what every agent is doing, its task, and its consumption (cost, API, time, output, efficiency), and push the product to be more autonomous and value-driven. The ask included an explicit invitation to critique the instinct and validate it against the canon (v10 and others).

**The read (operator + PM + marketer lenses).**

- **The trend is real and it is already our thesis.** Once "how to build" is cheap, the human's job becomes deciding, directing, and reviewing a fleet of agents. Cadence already casts the user this way: the PM decides what is worth building and watches it get built (v10), and the wedge is "be the manager, not the maker" (v9). "You are the agent manager" is the same idea in 2026's language: a narrative amplifier, not a pivot.

- **The trap is making it the category.** "Agent manager / agent ops / agent observability" is a crowding infrastructure category that sells to platform and engineering buyers on monitoring. If Cadence leads with "manage your agents," it (a) commoditizes itself into agent-ops, and (b) demotes the wedge that actually wins (judgment, red-teaming the roadmap, decision memory) and muddies the PM ICP. Reinforce the frame; do not repoint the category. Keep "the AI that red-teams your roadmap" as the lead and layer the operating-model frame underneath.

- **A literal in-app "Agent Manager" surface is the doctrine's named anti-pattern.** The Engine-Room Doctrine (calm front, deep engine; name the outcome, not the mechanism) calls this exact move "control-room creep." Cadence already has the surface, named for outcomes: the Engine Room (renamed from Govern) carries Activity, Analytics, Loop health, Spend, Quality checks, and Drift. Agent dispatch is Missions; per-run replay is Traces. The data plumbing (`ai_events`, `agent_runs` spend / tokens, `tool_calls`, `drift_snapshots`) is complete. So the founder's list is mostly already built; what is genuinely missing is one thing, cost-per-outcome / unit economics (backlog ENG-06), plus a tighter "manager's glance" of the fleet.

- **Audience split (founder call): both, by surface.** The end-user PM does not want to "manage agents"; they want to know what needs their judgment, what shipped, and what it cost for what they got. So cost is reframed from mechanism (API calls, tokens) to outcome (cost-per-outcome) and kept light on the calm front; the full per-agent telemetry stays behind the Engine Room door for the operator / founder.

- **The autonomous, value-driven form is management by exception.** In an autonomous system the manager's role is review and judgment, not micromanaging a live console. The product surfaces exceptions and decisions (what needs me, what is stuck, what shipped), with depth one click away. This is the autonomy on-ramp (observing to proving to trusted) doing its job, not a NASA control room.

**Decision (distilled).** Adopt agent-manager as a reinforcing narrative (landing page + how we talk about the role), not a category and not a new surface. Respect the Engine-Room Doctrine: no surface literally named "Agent Manager." Express cost as cost-per-outcome split across the calm front (light) and the Engine Room (deep). The build increment is the existing backlog item ENG-06, not new strategy. Distilled into [`v9`](./v9-decision-wedge-and-build-next.md) §2 (operating-model frame) and the [decisions log](./session-decisions.md); doctrine worked example added to [`../conventions/engine-room-doctrine.md`](../conventions/engine-room-doctrine.md).
